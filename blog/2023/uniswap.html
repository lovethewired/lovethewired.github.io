UniswapV2

The constant function AMM is defined through its invariant on the reserves. For Uniswap & co. the constant product formula x⋅y=kx \cdot y = kx⋅y=k is used.

Curve Invariant:

x⋅y=L2x \cdot y = L^2x⋅y=L2

Often liquidity LLL or, in this case L2L^2L2 is used in the invariant formula instead. Generally, the liquidity reflects how "liquid" the pool is, i.e. how many assets can be swapped and what the price impact is of a trade - a pool with deeper liquidity will have a slower rate of change in price.

Constant product AMMs are said to have "infinite liquidity", because the liquidity always remains the same during a swap. This means that it is always possible to swap with the pool, but the pool will return an increasingly smaller amount.

I can't say exactly why L2L^2L2 instead of simply LLL is used, buy it does seem to make sense, given that we are multiplying the amounts of two assets. And when providing liquidity, when keeping a constant price, the required tokens are proportional to LLL.

Price:

P=yxP = \frac y xP=xy​

The price PPP is defined as the ratio between the two reserves. Often, the price of an asset will be denominated in stable units. Given the pair ETH-USD, the xxx amount (token0) would reflect the ETH reserves and the yyy amount (token1) the USD reserves of a pool. The price PEth=yx=USDETHP_{\text{Eth}} = \frac y x = \frac {\text{USD}} {\text{ETH}}PEth​=xy​=ETHUSD​ is the dollar per Ether rate.

We made use of the fact that the liquidity LLL is constant.

This can be thought of the sensitivity of the output of a swap Δy\Delta yΔy given an infinitesimal amount of input Δx\Delta xΔx. If the price is low for example - this is when we are far right on the curve and the pool almost exclusively is comprised of XXX assets - the output will not be very sensitive to changes in xxx. Whereas when the price is high and the pool is almost exclusively made up of YYY assets, we'll only need a small amount of input Δx\Delta xΔx for a big change in output Δy\Delta yΔy.

UniswapV3

Virtual liquidity

The innovation in UniswapV3 comes from being able to concentrate our liquidity to a limited price range [pa,pb][p_a, p_b][pa​,pb​]. The original formula x⋅y=L2x \cdot y = L^2x⋅y=L2, allows anyone to trade with the pool at any price. By adjusting it, we can artificially inflate the assets, as if we had a deeper liquidity in a given price band. A deeper liquidity means that the rate of change of the price will be slower, but it also means that we are swapping more tokens when the price moves.

The way this is done is by adding constants to our real assets to make up our virtual assets. This corresponds to a translation/shift in the curve formula.

(xreal+sx)⋅(yreal+sy)=xvirtual⋅yvirtual=L2(x_\text {real} + s_x) \cdot (y_\text {real} + s_y) = x_\text {virtual} \cdot y_\text {virtual} = L^2(xreal​+sx​)⋅(yreal​+sy​)=xvirtual​⋅yvirtual​=L2

Here, xvirtualx_\text {virtual}xvirtual​ and yvirtualy_\text {virtual}yvirtual​ are the inflated, virtual assets and xrealx_\text {real}xreal​ and yrealy_\text {real}yreal​ reflect the real assets located in the pool.

The tradeoff for inflating our assets is that our liquidity is not infinite anymore - once we hit the upper or lower price limit, the liquidity for that range will be depleted and we will have entirely swapped one token for another. When working with the entire price range, there would always be assets left to swap.

When all of our liquidity is made up of only YYY assets for example, the amount of XXX assets will be zero xreal=0x_\text {real} = 0xreal​=0. The price of asset YYY per asset XXX then reaches the upper limit P=pbP=p_bP=pb​. Since we're using the inflated assets for our curve, the price is calculated using the virtual reserves xvirtualx_\text {virtual}xvirtual​ and yvirtualy_\text {virtual}yvirtual​.

Providing Liquidity

When providing liquidity, one condition we might want is for the price to remain constant. We can then easily express the change in tokens per change in liquidity ΔL\Delta LΔL, since the formulas are linearly dependent. A change in liquidity is proportional to a change in either XXX or YYY assets and vice versa.

Swapping

When swapping, the price changes according to the curve invariant, while we would like the (virtual) liquidity to remain constant. Instead of expressing the change in one asset through a change in the other, we can express the change in assets through the change in price instead. This is more convenient, as Uniswap stores the liquidity and price (or square root price) of assets instead of the absolute amounts.

Δx=L⋅Δ1P\Delta x = L \cdot \Delta \frac 1 {\sqrt P} \qquadΔx=L⋅ΔP​1​
Δy=L⋅ΔP\Delta y = L \cdot \Delta {\sqrt P} \qquadΔy=L⋅ΔP​

Swap XXX for YYY

Fees

feeGrowthGlobal fgf_{g}fg​ tracks the total amount of fees earned per unit of virtual liquidity for each token. These correspond to the fees earned for one unit of liquidity since the initialization of the protocol.

protocolFees fpf_pfp​ track the fees earned by the protocol (enabled and collected through governance). We'll ignore these for now.

When a swap is performed, the fees are applied to the input and the accumulated fees are increased:

fg=yin⋅γf_g = y_{in} \cdot \gammafg​=yin​⋅γ

NOTE: Shouldn't it be accounted per liquidity?

Δy=yin⋅(1−γ)\Delta y = y_{in} \cdot (1 - \gamma)Δy=yin​⋅(1−γ)

When calculating the fees owed to a liquidity provider's position, the delta in fee growth since the last update is multiplied by the liquidity size of the position. One thing to take into account is that a liquidity provider only earns fees for the range that they are providing liquidity for.
